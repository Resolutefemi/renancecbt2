Basics & Types of Data

Section 1 · Foundations

What is Statistics?

Definition

Statistics is the scientific method of collecting, organising, summarising, presenting, and analysing data, and making valid conclusions based on that analysis. It is applied in every field where numerical data exists — biology, economics, government, medicine, engineering, and beyond.

Descriptive Statistics: Concerned with summarising and describing the features of a dataset (tables, graphs, averages, spread). Does NOT generalise beyond the data collected.

Inferential Statistics: Uses sample data to draw conclusions (inferences) about a larger population. Includes hypothesis testing, regression, and confidence intervals.

Datum (singular) vs. Data (plural): A single recorded piece of information is a datum; multiple pieces are data.

Variable: Any characteristic that can take on different values across observations (e.g. height, gender, marks). A constant does not vary.

Types of Variables

Qualitative vs. Quantitative

Qualitative (Categorical) variables describe attributes or categories that cannot be measured numerically — e.g. blood group, gender, religion, marital status, eye colour.
Quantitative (Numerical) variables take numeric values on which arithmetic operations are meaningful — e.g. height, weight, age, income.

Key Distinction: Discrete vs. Continuous
  • Discrete: Values obtained by counting; no fractions possible (e.g. you cannot have 2.5 children). Examples: number of cars in a lot, number of students absent.
  • Continuous: Values obtained by measuring; any value (including fractions/decimals) is possible between two points. Examples: weight (60.5 kg), time, height.
  • Important: "Age of a student in class" is continuous — age can be measured to infinite precision. "Number of children born" is discrete.

Scales of Measurement (NOIR)

① Nominal
Operations: = ≠ only

Most basic level. Numbers are labels only — no order, no arithmetic. Classifies items into mutually exclusive categories.

Examples: Gender, Religion, Blood group, Nationality, Zip codes, Marital status

② Ordinal
Operations: = ≠ < >

Has order/rank, but intervals between ranks are NOT equal or known. Cannot say "the 1st person is twice as tall as the 2nd".

Examples: Class rank (1st, 2nd), Degree class, Pain scale (mild/moderate/severe), Likert scale

③ Interval
Operations: = ≠ < > + −

Equal intervals between values. BUT no true zero — zero does not mean "absence". Addition and subtraction are valid; ratios are not.

Examples: Temperature (°C, °F), IQ score, Calendar year, Clock time

④ Ratio
Operations: = ≠ < > + − × ÷

Highest level. Equal intervals AND a true zero (zero = absence of the attribute). ALL arithmetic operations are valid; ratios are meaningful.

Examples: Height, Weight, Income, Distance, Temperature (Kelvin), Speed

Memory Aid

Remember NOIR: Nominal → Ordinal → Interval → Ratio. Each level includes all properties of the levels below it. "Discrete" is a type of quantitative variable, NOT a level of measurement.

Types of Data: Primary vs. Secondary

Primary Data
First-hand / Original
Collected directly by the investigator for the first time, specifically for the study at hand. The researcher is involved at every stage.
✔ Advantages: Investigator has confidence in data; comprehensive; definition of terms included; more reliable for specific needs.
✘ Disadvantages: Time-consuming; expensive; requires considerable manpower; data may be obsolete by the time of publication.
Secondary Data
Existing / Published
Obtained from existing published or unpublished sources — journals, government records, statistical divisions, banks, newspapers, research institutions.
✔ Advantages: Cheap and fast to collect; easily available; good for historical analysis.
✘ Disadvantages: May be misrepresented; may not fit the researcher's exact needs; precision/reliability may be unknown; may contain transcription errors.

Key Terminology

Population, Sample, Parameter, Statistic
  • Population: The entire group of objects/individuals about which information is sought. Can be finite or infinite.
  • Sample: A subset of the population selected for study. Must be representative.
  • Parameter (μ, σ, etc.): A numerical summary computed from a population (e.g. population mean μ). Fixed but often unknown.
  • Statistic (x̄, s, etc.): A numerical summary computed from a sample (e.g. sample mean x̄). Used to estimate parameters.
  • Random Variable: A variable whose value is determined by the outcome of a random experiment. Can be discrete or continuous.
  • Census: A complete count/data collection from every member of the population.
  • Sampling: The process of selecting a representative subset (sample) from a population — used because a census is often impossible or too expensive.

Data Presentation & Tabulation

Section 2 · Graphs, Charts & Tables

Statistical Tables

Components of a Standard Statistical Table
  • General Title: Overall heading at the top describing the table's content.
  • Caption: Column headings — describe what each column represents.
  • Stub: Row headings — labels on the left side of the table.
  • Body: The main area containing actual numerical data values.
  • Source Note: Indicates where the data was obtained (attribution).
  • Footnote: Provides additional explanations, qualifications, or clarifications.
NOT a Table Requirement

Bright colors, embedded graphs, and decorative elements are NOT structural requirements of a statistical table. A table requires: Title, Caption, Stub, Body, and Source Note.

Frequency Distribution Tables

Key Concepts
  • Frequency (f): Count of observations in each class. Total = Σf = n.
  • Relative Frequency: Class frequency ÷ Total frequency (f/n). All relative frequencies sum to 1.
  • Cumulative Frequency: Running total of frequencies up to (and including) each class.
  • Tally Marks: Used to count occurrences of each value when building a frequency table.
  • Class Limits: The stated lowest and highest values of each class (e.g. 10–19).
  • Class Boundaries: Adjusted limits obtained by subtracting/adding 0.5 to limits (e.g. 9.5–19.5). Used to eliminate gaps.
  • Class Midpoint (Class Mark): (Lower limit + Upper limit) / 2. Used in calculations for grouped data.
  • Class Width (c): Upper boundary − Lower boundary.
  • Open-ended Class: First or last class with no stated boundary (e.g. "below 10" or "100 and above").
Class Width

Class Width Formula

c = Upper Boundary − Lower Boundary c = Range ÷ Number of Classes (k)
e.g. For class 10–19: boundaries are 9.5–19.5, so c = 19.5 − 9.5 = 10.
Class Midpoint

Class Mark / Midpoint

x = (Lower Limit + Upper Limit) / 2
e.g. For class 47–50: midpoint = (47 + 50) / 2 = 48.5.
Relative Frequency

Relative Frequency

rf = f / Σf = f / n
Σ(all relative frequencies) = 1. Expressed as a proportion or percentage.
Number of Classes

Sturge's Rule (Number of Classes)

k ≈ √n (or k = 1 + 3.322 × log₁₀(n))
For n = 100: k = √100 = 10. For n = 200: k ≈ 14. For n = 400: k ≈ 20.
Frequency Density

Frequency Density (Unequal Classes)

d = f / c (frequency ÷ class width)
Used as the y-axis in histograms with unequal class widths. Area of each bar = frequency.
Pie Chart Angle

Pie Chart Sector Angle

θ = (f / Σf) × 360°
Total of all angles = 360°. For relative frequency 0.4: angle = 0.4 × 360° = 144°.

Histogram vs. Bar Chart — Critical Distinctions
  • Histogram: No gaps between bars (continuous data); area of bar ∝ frequency; y-axis can show frequency or frequency density.
  • Bar Chart: Gaps between bars (discrete/categorical data); height of bar = frequency; width has no meaning.
  • A histogram is applicable only to continuous data. Bar charts are for qualitative or discrete data.
  • The modal class in a histogram is the tallest bar (highest frequency density).
Ogive — How to Use It
  • A "less than" ogive plots cumulative frequency against the upper class boundary.
  • A "more than" ogive plots cumulative frequency against the lower class boundary.
  • The ogive starts at zero (lower boundary of first class, cumulative f = 0) and ends at n (last upper boundary).
  • To find the median: draw a horizontal line at n/2 on the y-axis; read the x-value.
  • To find quartiles: Q1 at n/4, Q2 (median) at n/2, Q3 at 3n/4.
  • For percentages, convert y-axis to % (multiply by 100/n); median is at 50%.

Classification & Tabulation Purpose

Why We Classify & Tabulate Data
  • Condense large amounts of raw data into an understandable form.
  • Facilitate comparisons between groups or variables.
  • Enhance clarity of pictorial/graphical representation.
  • Enable calculation of averages, spread, and other statistics.
  • Make data presentation clear, systematic, and informative.

Measures of Central Tendency

Section 3 · Mean, Median, Mode, GM, HM, Weighted Mean

Overview

What is a Measure of Central Tendency?

A single value that represents the centre or typical value of a dataset — also called an average. The word "average" refers to the centre of a data set. The three most common are: Mean, Median, and Mode.

① Arithmetic Mean (x̄ or μ)

Ungrouped Data

Simple Arithmetic Mean

x̄ = Σx / n
x̄: sample mean  |  Σx: sum of all values  |  n: number of observations.
If mean = M and total count = n, then Σx = n × M.
Grouped Data

Mean for Grouped / Frequency Distribution

x̄ = Σfx / Σf
f: class frequency  |  x: class midpoint  |  Σf = n.
Assumed Mean Method

Coding / Assumed Mean Method

x̄ = A + (Σfd / Σf) where d = x − A (deviation) For equal class widths: x̄ = A + (Σfd' / Σf) × c where d' = (x − A) / c
A: assumed mean (chosen near the centre)  |  c: class width. Reduces arithmetic by using smaller deviations.
Combined / Weighted Mean

Combined Mean of Two Groups

x̄_combined = (n₁x̄₁ + n₂x̄₂) / (n₁ + n₂) Weighted Mean: x̄_w = Σwx / Σw
n₁, n₂: group sizes  |  w: weights. Used when groups have different sizes or importance.
Properties of the Arithmetic Mean
  • Uses every data value in its calculation — sensitive to all values.
  • Σ(x − x̄) = 0 — the sum of all deviations from the mean is always zero.
  • Σ(x − x̄)² is minimized — the mean minimizes the sum of squared deviations (least squares property).
  • If a constant k is added to all values, the new mean = x̄ + k. (Spread unchanged.)
  • If all values are multiplied by k, the new mean = k × x̄.
  • The mean is not appropriate for open-ended classes, qualitative data, or when extreme outliers are present.
Disadvantages of Arithmetic Mean
  • Highly affected by outliers (extreme values) — can give a misleading "average".
  • Not meaningful for qualitative (categorical) data.
  • Cannot be computed for open-ended frequency distributions without assumptions.
  • May produce a non-physical value (e.g. 2.3 children).

Trimmed Mean

Trimmed Mean

The α% trimmed mean is computed by: (1) sorting all values, (2) discarding α% of the smallest and α% of the largest values, (3) computing the arithmetic mean of the remaining values. This makes it robust to outliers.

  • α = 0% → same as arithmetic mean.
  • α = 50% → same as the median.
  • Example: 20% trimmed mean of 5 values (32, 10, 8, 9, 11): discard 1 smallest (8) and 1 largest (32); mean of 9, 10, 11 = 10.
  • Used by judges in competitions (gymnastics, diving) — discard highest and lowest scores.

② Median

Ungrouped Data

Median — Ungrouped

If n is ODD: Median = x_{(n+1)/2} If n is EVEN: Median = (x_{n/2} + x_{n/2+1}) / 2
Always arrange data in ascending order first. For n = 7: Median = 4th value. For n = 6: Median = average of 3rd and 4th values.
Grouped Data

Median — Grouped Data (Interpolation)

Median = L_m + [(n/2 − F_c) / f_m] × c Where: L_m = lower boundary of median class n = total frequency (Σf) F_c = cumulative freq. BEFORE median class f_m = frequency of median class c = class width
The median class is the class where cumulative frequency first equals or exceeds n/2.
Properties of the Median
  • Divides an ordered dataset into two equal halves — 50% below, 50% above.
  • Not affected by outliers — robust measure; best for skewed data.
  • Minimizes the sum of absolute deviations: Σ|x − M| is minimized at M = median.
  • Equivalent to: Q2 (2nd quartile), D5 (5th decile), P50 (50th percentile).
  • Can be estimated graphically from the ogive at the 50% cumulative frequency level.
  • In grouped data, the median is an estimate (interpolation), not an exact value.

③ Mode

Ungrouped Data

Mode — Ungrouped

Mode = value that occurs most frequently
A dataset can be: Unimodal (1 mode), Bimodal (2 modes), Multimodal (3+ modes), or have No mode (all frequencies equal).
Grouped Data

Mode — Grouped Data

Mode = L_m + [(f_m − f_b) / ((f_m−f_b)+(f_m−f_a))] × c Where: L_m = lower boundary of modal class f_m = frequency of modal class (highest f) f_b = frequency of class BEFORE modal class f_a = frequency of class AFTER modal class c = class width
The modal class is the class with the highest frequency. Mode can also be estimated from the histogram — the tallest bar.
Properties of the Mode
  • Only measure of central tendency usable for nominal (qualitative) data.
  • Not affected by extreme values (outliers).
  • May not be unique (multiple modes possible) or may not exist at all.
  • Does not use all data values — only depends on frequency counts.
  • Estimated graphically from the histogram (tallest bar).

④ Geometric Mean (GM)

Formula

Geometric Mean

GM = (x₁ × x₂ × … × xₙ)^(1/n) = ⁿ√(x₁ · x₂ · … · xₙ) For 2 values: GM = √(a × b) For 3 values: GM = ³√(a × b × c)
e.g. GM of 4 and 16: √(4×16) = √64 = 8.  |  GM of 4 and 9: √36 = 6.
When to Use GM & Its Properties
  • Best for rates, ratios, percentages, index numbers, and growth rates (multiplicative data).
  • Cannot be computed if any value is zero or negative (product becomes 0 or imaginary).
  • Always ≤ AM for positive unequal numbers: HM ≤ GM ≤ AM.
  • Used for averaging compound interest rates and economic growth rates.

⑤ Harmonic Mean (HM)

Formula

Harmonic Mean

HM = n / Σ(1/xᵢ) = n / (1/x₁ + 1/x₂ + … + 1/xₙ)
e.g. HM of 2 and 3: 2 ÷ (1/2 + 1/3) = 2 ÷ (5/6) = 2.4.  |  Best for rates (speed, productivity).
Ordering of Means

For any set of positive, unequal numbers:  HM ≤ GM ≤ AM.  Equality holds only when all values are identical. This is called the AM-GM-HM inequality.

Empirical Relationship (Moderately Skewed Data)

Empirical Relation

Mean – Median – Mode Relationship

Mean − Mode = 3 × (Mean − Median) ∴ Mode ≈ 3 × Median − 2 × Mean
Valid only for moderately skewed distributions. Used to estimate Mode when Median and Mean are known, or to find Median from Mean and Mode.
Example: Mean = 25, Mode = 19 → 25−19 = 3(25−Median) → Median = 23.

Dispersion, Skewness & Kurtosis

Section 4 · Spread, Shape & Distribution Characteristics

What is Dispersion?

Definition

A measure of dispersion (variability/spread) indicates how much individual values differ from each other and from the central value. If dispersion = 0, all values are identical and the mean perfectly represents every value. Greater dispersion = less representative mean.

① Range

Simplest Measure

Range

R = X_max − X_min (Largest − Smallest)
Advantage: Simple to calculate. Disadvantage: Uses only 2 extreme values — highly sensitive to outliers. Range ≥ IQR always.
Mid-range

Mid-Range

Mid-range = (X_max + X_min) / 2
The average of the two extreme values. Simple to compute but not a robust measure.

② Quartile Deviation (Semi-IQR)

IQR & QD

Inter-Quartile Range & Quartile Deviation

IQR = Q3 − Q1 (middle 50% of data) Quartile Deviation (QD) = (Q3 − Q1) / 2 = Semi-IQR Coefficient of QD = (Q3 − Q1) / (Q3 + Q1)
IQR is the most robust measure of dispersion — unaffected by outliers. Covers the middle 50% of data. Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5×IQR; Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5×IQR.

③ Mean Deviation (MD)

Ungrouped

Mean Absolute Deviation

MD = Σ|x − x̄| / n For frequency distribution: MD = Σf|x − x̄| / Σf
Uses absolute values to prevent cancellation of positive and negative deviations. MD is always ≤ SD. Minimized when deviations are taken from the median.

④ Variance & Standard Deviation

Population

Population Variance (σ²)

σ² = Σ(x − μ)² / N = Σx²/N − (Σx/N)² (shortcut) σ = √σ² (Population SD)
Uses N in denominator. μ = population mean. σ² in squared units; σ in original units.
Sample

Sample Variance (s²) — Bessel's Correction

s² = Σ(x − x̄)² / (n−1) = [Σx² − (Σx)²/n] / (n−1) s = √s² (Sample SD)
Uses n−1 (Bessel's correction) for an unbiased estimate of population variance. Example: for sample {1,2,3} → mean=2, s²=[(1+0+1)/(3−1)] = 1.
Grouped Data

Variance — Grouped Frequency Distribution

σ² = Σf(x − x̄)² / (N−1) = [Σfx² / Σf] − (Σfx/Σf)² (shortcut)
x = class midpoint, f = class frequency, N = Σf.
Relative Measure

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

CV = (s / x̄) × 100%
Unit-free relative measure — used to compare variability of datasets with different units or different means. Lower CV = more consistent data. CV of 5% is far less variable than CV of 40%.
Key Properties of Variance & Standard Deviation
  • SD is always ≥ 0. SD = 0 if and only if all values are identical.
  • SD is in the same units as the original data; variance is in squared units.
  • Adding a constant to all values: SD unchanged (shift doesn't change spread).
  • Multiplying all values by k: SD multiplied by |k|; variance multiplied by k².
  • SD minimizes the sum of squared deviations: Σ(x − c)² is minimized when c = mean.
  • The squared deviations are minimized at the mean; absolute deviations are minimized at the median.
  • For data {2,4,4,4,5,5,7,9}: mean=5, σ²=4, σ=2.

Box Plot (Box-and-Whisker Plot)

Five-Number Summary & Box Plot
  • A box plot displays the five-number summary: Minimum, Q1, Median (Q2), Q3, Maximum.
  • The box spans from Q1 to Q3 — its length = IQR.
  • The median line is drawn inside the box at Q2.
  • Whiskers extend from the box to the most extreme non-outlier values (within 1.5×IQR of Q1 and Q3).
  • Mild outliers: Between 1.5×IQR and 3×IQR from the quartiles — shown as open circles.
  • Extreme outliers: Beyond 3×IQR from the quartiles — shown as filled circles.
  • Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5×IQR  |  Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5×IQR.
  • Box skewed toward Q1 → right skew; box skewed toward Q3 → left skew.

Skewness

What is Skewness?

Skewness measures the degree of asymmetry (departure from symmetry) of a distribution. A perfectly symmetric distribution has skewness = 0.

Positive Skew (Right)
Mean > Median > Mode

Long tail to the RIGHT. The mean is pulled furthest right. Outliers are on the upper end.

Symmetric (Zero Skew)
Mean = Median = Mode

Perfect bell shape. Both halves are mirror images. Skewness = 0.

Negative Skew (Left)
Mode > Median > Mean

Long tail to the LEFT. The mean is pulled furthest left. Outliers are on the lower end.

Moment-Based

Skewness (Pearson's Moment Coefficient)

Skewness = [Σ(xᵢ − x̄)³ / (n−1)] / σ³
Positive → right skew | Zero → symmetric | Negative → left skew. |Skewness| > 1 = highly skewed.
Pearson's 1st

Pearson's First Coefficient of Skewness

Sk₁ = (Mean − Mode) / SD
Uses mode. When mode is not well-defined, use Pearson's second coefficient.
Pearson's 2nd

Pearson's Second Coefficient of Skewness

Sk₂ = 3(Mean − Median) / SD
More stable than Sk₁. Positive → right skew | Zero → symmetric | Negative → left skew.

Kurtosis

What is Kurtosis?

Kurtosis measures the peakedness (sharpness) of a distribution relative to the normal curve. It describes whether data values concentrate around the peak (heavy tails) or are spread broadly (light tails).

Leptokurtic
Kurtosis > 3 | Excess > 0

More peaked than normal. Sharper, thinner peak; heavier tails. "Lepto" = thin/sharp.

Mesokurtic
Kurtosis = 3 | Excess = 0

Same peakedness as normal distribution. The standard normal curve is mesokurtic.

Platykurtic
Kurtosis < 3 | Excess < 0

Flatter than normal. Broader, flatter peak; lighter tails. "Platy" = flat/broad.

Excess Kurtosis

Excess Kurtosis = Kurtosis − 3. The normal distribution has excess kurtosis = 0. Leptokurtic: excess > 0. Platykurtic: excess < 0. Software (Excel, R, SPSS) typically reports excess kurtosis.

Quantiles, Deciles & Percentiles

Section 5 · Partitioning Distributions

Quantile Overview

What Are Quantiles?

Quantiles are values that partition a distribution into equal-sized groups. The key families are: Quartiles (4 groups), Deciles (10 groups), and Percentiles (100 groups).

Quartiles (Q)
3 values split distribution into 4 equal parts.
Q1 = 25th %ile (P25)
Q2 = Median = 50th %ile (P50)
Q3 = 75th %ile (P75)
Deciles (D)
9 values split distribution into 10 equal parts.
D1 = P10  |  D5 = Median = P50
D9 = P90
D5 = Q2 = Median
Percentiles (P)
99 values split distribution into 100 equal parts.
P50 = Median = Q2 = D5
P25 = Q1  |  P75 = Q3
P10 = D1  |  P90 = D9

Formulas for Grouped Data

Quartiles

Q1, Q2, Q3 — Grouped Data

Q₁ = L₁ + [(n/4 − F_c) / f₁] × c Q₂ = L₂ + [(n/2 − F_c) / f₂] × c Q₃ = L₃ + [(3n/4 − F_c) / f₃] × c IQR = Q3 − Q1
Find the class: cumulative frequency first ≥ n/4 for Q1; ≥ n/2 for Q2; ≥ 3n/4 for Q3.
Deciles

Deciles — Grouped Data

Dₖ = Lₖ + [(kn/10 − F_c) / fₖ] × c where k = 1, 2, …, 9
D1 class: cum. freq ≥ n/10. D9 class: cum. freq ≥ 9n/10. Variables same as median formula.
Percentiles

Percentiles — Grouped Data

Pₖ = Lₖ + [(kn/100 − F_c) / fₖ] × c where k = 1, 2, …, 99
P50 = Q2 = D5 = Median. Position of Pₖ for ungrouped: k(n+1)/100.

Graphical Estimation Using Ogive
  • To find Q1 on ogive: draw horizontal line at 25% (or n/4); read x-value. Q1 → 25% level.
  • To find Q2 (Median): horizontal line at 50%.
  • To find Q3: horizontal line at 75%.
  • To find D4: horizontal line at 40%.
  • To find P10: horizontal line at 10%.
  • Rule: Quantile level (%) = (k/divisor) × 100. For Q1: (1/4)×100 = 25%; for D9: (9/10)×100 = 90%; for P30: 30%.

Probability & Normal Distribution

Section 6 · Probability Basics, Normal Curve & Standardisation

Basic Probability

Fundamentals

Basic Probability Rules

0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1 (always) P(impossible) = 0 P(certain) = 1 Σ P(all outcomes) = 1 Complement: P(A') = 1 − P(A)
Mutually Exclusive Events: P(A and B) = 0; cannot occur simultaneously.
Independent Events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).
Random Variable
  • A random variable is a variable whose value is determined by the outcome of a random experiment.
  • Discrete random variable: countable outcomes (e.g. number of heads in 10 coin tosses).
  • Continuous random variable: any value in an interval (e.g. time to complete an exam, weight, height).

The Normal Distribution

Properties of the Normal Distribution
  • Also called the Gaussian (bell) curve. Fully described by mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ).
  • Symmetric about the mean: Mean = Median = Mode.
  • Bell-shaped: unimodal, approaches (but never touches) x-axis asymptotically.
  • Total area under curve = 1.
  • Mesokurtic: kurtosis = 3 (excess kurtosis = 0).
  • Skewness = 0 (perfectly symmetric).
  • NOT all symmetric distributions are bell-shaped.

  The Empirical Rule (68–95–99.7 Rule)

68%μ ± 1σ
95%μ ± 2σ
99.7%μ ± 3σ
Standardisation

Z-Score (Standard Score)

z = (x − μ) / σ Inverse: x = μ + z·σ
Z-score = number of standard deviations from the mean. z = 0 means x = μ. z = +2 means x is 2 SDs above mean. The Standard Normal Distribution: μ = 0, σ = 1.
Central Limit Theorem

Standard Error of the Mean

SE = σ / √n
For large n, the sampling distribution of x̄ approaches normality (Central Limit Theorem). The standard error is the SD of the sampling distribution of the mean.

Sampling Methods & Theory

Section 7 · Probability & Non-Probability Sampling

Why Sample?

Rationale for Sampling

A sample survey collects data from a subset of the population. Sampling is used because a census (studying every unit) is often impractical, too expensive, too time-consuming, or impossible. A good sample must be representative — it must accurately reflect the population's characteristics.

Key Sampling Terms
  • Sampling Frame: A complete, up-to-date list of all population units from which the sample is drawn. Required for SRS.
  • Sampling Fraction: n/N (sample size ÷ population size).
  • Sampling Interval (k): N/n — used in systematic sampling.
  • Sampling Error: Difference between sample statistic and population parameter (x̄ − μ). Decreases as sample size increases.
  • Non-Sampling Error: Errors from non-response, measurement mistakes, and bias — NOT reduced by larger samples.
  • Bias: A systematic tendency to over- or under-estimate the true population value.

Probability Sampling Methods

Simple Random Sampling
Probability • Gold Standard
Every unit in the population has an equal probability (1/N) of selection. Selection uses a lottery or random number table. Eliminates selection bias.
✔ Pros: Unbiased; equal probability; highest precision among probability methods. No personal bias.
✘ Cons: Requires complete sampling frame; impractical/expensive for large, dispersed populations; time-consuming.
Systematic Sampling
Probability • Fixed Interval
Select first unit randomly (r, where 1 ≤ r ≤ k), then select every kth unit thereafter. Interval k = N/n. No full sampling frame needed.
✔ Pros: Easy to use; sample spread evenly; faster than SRS; no complete frame needed.
✘ Cons: If list has a periodic pattern coinciding with k, results will be biased. Not fully random after first selection.
Stratified Sampling
Probability • Subgroups
Population divided into strata (sub-groups) that are internally homogeneous but differ from each other. Random sample taken from each stratum.
✔ Pros: More representative; higher precision; all subgroups guaranteed representation; useful for heterogeneous populations.
✘ Cons: Requires prior knowledge of strata; complex to execute; needs up-to-date frame per stratum; costly if strata frames unavailable.
Cluster Sampling
Probability • Geographic Groups
Population divided into clusters (heterogeneous groups). Clusters are randomly selected; ALL units within selected clusters are studied. Works without a complete frame.
✔ Pros: Cost-effective for geographically dispersed populations; no complete frame needed; reduces travel costs.
✘ Cons: If units within clusters are similar (homogeneous), results are less precise. Higher sampling error than SRS with same n.
Stratified vs. Cluster — Critical Distinction
  • Stratified: Strata are internally homogeneous (similar within), externally heterogeneous (different between strata). All strata sampled.
  • Cluster: Clusters are internally heterogeneous (diverse within). Only selected clusters are studied; all members of chosen clusters are surveyed.

Non-Probability Sampling Methods

Non-Probability Sampling

In non-probability sampling, the probability of each unit being selected is unknown. Selection depends on human judgment or availability. Cannot calculate sampling error or guarantee representativeness. Generally avoided in rigorous research.

Quota Sampling
Non-Probability
Pre-assigns quotas (fixed numbers) to subgroups (like stratification), but selection within quotas is non-random — left to interviewer's discretion.
✔ Pros: Cheaper and faster than stratified; introduces some representation.
✘ Cons: Interviewer bias in selection; not truly random; cannot measure sampling error.
Convenience / Accidental
Non-Probability
Selects whoever is most easily available at the time. No systematic procedure. First persons encountered are selected.
✔ Pros: Very quick and cheap; no planning required.
✘ Cons: Highly biased; not representative; results cannot be generalized. Most likely to produce a biased sample.
Purposive / Judgmental
Non-Probability
Researcher uses expert knowledge/judgment to select specific units believed to be representative.
✔ Pros: Can be useful for specific, expert-driven purposes; quick.
✘ Cons: Variability and bias cannot be measured; depends entirely on researcher's accuracy of judgment.

Data Collection Methods

Section 8 · Questionnaire, Interview, Observation, Documentary

Overview

Four Primary Methods

Data collection choice depends on: aims/objectives of the survey, nature of information needed, target population, required accuracy, available time, and cost. The four main methods are: Questionnaire, Interview, Observation, and Documentary.

Data Collection Method Comparison

Questionnaire Method
Primary Data
Specially designed forms sent to respondents to answer and return. Can be postal, online, or hand-distributed. The most widely used method.
✔ Pros: Wide geographic coverage; saves time; low cost; collects diverse information; respondents can answer at their convenience.
✘ Cons: Low response rates; risk of incomplete/inaccurate answers; risk of false information; questionnaire design must be error-free (avoid ambiguous/leading questions).
Interview Method
Primary Data
Face-to-face or telephone verbal exchange between interviewer and respondent. Allows direct clarification and follow-up.
✔ Pros: Allows free interaction; more detailed information; interviewer can correct false answers; high response rate.
✘ Cons: Limited coverage area; time-consuming for large samples; expensive (travel costs); interviewer bias risk; appointment required.
Observation Method
Primary Data
Systematic, scientific watching and recording of events as they happen in their natural setting. Used in controlled experiments and field studies.
✔ Pros: High accuracy (data from actual events); no distortion from respondent memory; observer can control the process.
✘ Cons: Very time-consuming (must wait for event); expensive; observer effect (Hawthorne effect); limited to observable behaviours.
Documentary Method
Secondary Data
Uses existing published or unpublished records — books, reports, newspapers, official registers, government statistics, research publications.
✔ Pros: Saves time and money; provides historical data; less labor intensive; provides official information.
✘ Cons: Errors in original documents are inherited; false/inaccurate records difficult to correct; may not match researcher's needs exactly.

Questionnaire Design Rules

Key Rules for Good Questionnaire Design
  • Avoid ambiguous questions (unclear meaning) and leading questions (suggest the answer).
  • Pilot test the questionnaire on a small group before full deployment.
  • Questions should be logically arranged, clear, and concise.
  • Closed-ended questions (fixed response options) are easier to analyse; open-ended allow free responses.
  • A Likert scale (Strongly Agree → Strongly Disagree) produces ordinal data.
  • Structured questionnaire: Pre-defined questions with fixed response options.

Uses of Statistics — Summary

Industry & Business: Market research, feasibility studies, quality control, investment decisions, economic forecasting, auditing.

Biological Sciences: Crop yield analysis, animal diet studies, medical trials, public health advancement.

Physical Sciences: Astronomy, meteorology, geology, oil exploration — sampling and lab experiments.

Government: Population census, budget planning, health and defense policy, revenue estimation. Government is the most important user AND producer of statistical data.

Economics: Predicting market trends, analysing inflation, modelling economic growth, constructing index numbers.
